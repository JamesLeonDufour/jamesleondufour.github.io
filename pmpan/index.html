PROTECTION MONITORING 

PANAMA

Introduction and Context

Panama is home to +15,000 refugees and asylum-seekers. The refugee population is mainly composed of persons from Colombia, El Salvador, Cuba, Nicaragua, and Venezuela. During the COVID-19 pandemic, refugees and asylum seekers in Panama have faced new and exacerbated risks; especially with the loss of opportunities to generate an income, daily subsistence has become a challenge.

In March of 2020, the Panamanian government adopted the Cabinet Resolution No. 11, through which the National Emergency State was declared as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic. Restrictions on mobility and other sanitary measures were adopted as a means of preventing the spread of the disease and further contagion of the population within the national territory.

UNHCR conducted a protection monitoring during the COVID-19 pandemic context, emphasizing the impact and consequences that the restrictions adopted had on refugees and asylum seekers. The results shed light on the protection risks faced by the persons of concern (PoCs) due to the limited access to rights and services during the pandemic context.

The protection monitoring was conducted remotely via phone interviews to 435 households from October 21st to December 31st, 2020. The questionnaire addressed the respondents’ access to basic needs and services, access to rights, coping mechanisms, and main impacts of the COVID-19 health crisis on the households’ overall situation.

Profile of Respondents 

Nationality

The main nationalities represented though the 435 conducted interviews were Venezuelan (42%), Colombian (24%), Nicaraguan (21%), Salvadorian (7%), Cuban (4%); other nationalities (Honduran and extracontinental) represented 2% of the total number of surveyed persons.

Represented Age Groups

Approximately a quarter (24%) of the surveyed persons did not report the incidence of any specific need in the household.

Legal Status

Interviewees who reported holding a temporary residence visa or permit included asylum seekers admitted to the procedure, recognized refugees and persons who had a temporary permit issued under the extraordinary migratory regularization process better known as “Crisol de Razas” . The majority of surveyed persons who declared to have a permanent residence visa or permit in Panama were recognized refugees. Respondents who stated not holding a valid visa or legal status in Panama represented cases of denied refugee status determination (RSD) applications or arrivals to Panama who could not access a legal status process during the required timeframe, either under the RSD procedure or a migratory visa application

Area of Residence

Protection Incidents

A higher proportion of reports were related to incidents that occurred in the country of origin, which showed the main reasons for the displacement of the respondents; however, a high percentage of interviewees reported to have been a victim or witness of risk situations also in Panama. Compared to the results of the PMT 2019 protection monitoring, there was an increased rate of high violence crimes witnessed in Panama, such as armed robbery, harassment and/or sexual assault, physical assault, among others. The surveyed persons who reported incidents in Panama generally expressed they had not presented the pertinent complaints, because many times the perpetrator was a member of the same community or knew the victim; on occasions, the interviewees received threats not to continue with legal processes regarding the cases that occurred. In other instances, the perpetrators identified by the surveyed persons were part of the security forces, mainly the National Police, for which the victims commented that they did not have the necessary sense of protection to make the corresponding complaints.

Few respondents reported threats or incidents that happened in Panama related with their country of origin persecution agent or have identified persons in Panama linked with the persecution agent. Others declared to have received threats in Panama from the persecution agent located in the country of origin. 

Priority Needs 

Surveyed persons were asked to identify their highest priority need for the previous month, considering the COVID-19 context and the related restrictions and/or imposed sanitary measures. It was common of interviewees to state that all provided answer options represented pressing matters; however, many pointed out the need of documentation and regularization of their status in the country in order to be able to cover all other need.

This information showed a shift in the priority needs of the surveyed persons in comparison to the needs identified in the June-December 2019 Protection Monitoring Tool (PMT) report, which marked legal status regularization and documents as the main need (40%); secondly, work access, health, education, legal assistance among others (33.5%); and lastly, shelter, food, cash based interventions (23%).

Access to Documentation 

Although there have been advancements and improvements in the timeframe related to the RSD procedure, the Panamanian legislation does not grant access to a work permit while the asylum seeker’s case is at the admissibility phase of the process; this implies that PoCs who are in the first stage of the RSD process have access mainly to informal income generating activities (informal/occasional employment, street or home-based sales, day labor).

In some of the cases, interviewees declared to have accessed a temporary residence visa or permit through the extraordinary migratory process known as “Crisol de Razas” or by means of the humanitarian visa; those who chose this option of regularization might have had their RSD application rejected, might have not applied for the RSD procedure in Panama, or might have abandoned the RSD procedure due to extended waiting time and lack of access to a work permit. In other cases, respondents reported they had not had the resources to apply to any of the migratory regularization processes available in the country because of the high costs they entail, opting thus for an irregular stay in Panama without holding any type of residence visa or permit.

Asylum seekers in Panama receive as documentation issued by ONPAR a sheet containing the QR code that displays the process details when scanned. This document is often questioned by National Police officers and even officials of the National Immigration Service.

Access to Food

The COVID-19 pandemic and national context had impacted greatly on the interviewees’ ability to meet basic needs. Before the measures adopted by the Panamanian government in response to the COVID-19 crisis, 90% of the surveyed persons declared to have had access to three or more meals a day; 10% could eat two meals daily, while only 1% could ensure a single meal a day. 

At the time of the survey, a drastic shift in food consumption trends could be noticed, in that more than half (55%) of the surveyed households only had access to two meals daily; one-third of the interviewees declared that their household could eat three or more meals daily. The percentage of those who had access to a single meal a day rouse to 13% of the surveyed persons.

Coping Mechanisms

The most reported coping mechanism during the COVID-19 context to which interviewees resorted to cover basic needs of the household was the reduction in the quantity or quality of the food eaten by the family (92%). Decrease in non-essential food purchases (such as produce, dairy, or high-quality protein such as chicken or lean meat) was the second most recurred to activity to ensure coverage of basic needs of the household, reported by 89% of interviewees. Finally, 51% of the surveyed sample (which represented roughly 83% of the families with children) declared that they restricted food consumption amongst the adults in order to prioritize the children in the family.

Respondents reported that, due to household income decrease, they had not been able to provide the family with a balanced diet including fresh produce or dairy. Moreover, many households depended during the COVID-19 context on social or humanitarian aid from the government and/or other organizations and entities. When the assistance was provided in the form of food items, it mostly included dry food (rice, pasta, flour, beans), canned protein (such as tuna fish), and processed products (such as hot dogs). This dietary situation could have aggravated the medical conditions present in surveyed persons with chronic diseases and could have impacted the developmental processes of young children.

For cases where aid was declared to have been received from the Government, the majority of interviewees reported to have been benefited only during the onset of the crisis, during the first months of the pandemic (April, May, June); most of them reported to have stopped receiving assistance from the State once the “Panama Solidario” plan was launched .

Respondents also opted for requesting access to humanitarian aid at their local government authorities, such as communal boards (Juntas Comunales), but in many cases the assistance was never received. In other cases, local authorities’ delivered aid to the community, but it was denied to the interviewees because they presented foreign identification documents (passports, mainly) to the government officials. Thus, the declared perception of the surveyed persons was that “there is no aid for foreigners from the Government, as aid is only for Panamanians” .

Access to Housing and Basic Living Services

Housing Type

Cuban nationals made up the largest proportion of respondents renting out single rooms, as in most cases their households did not have children. However, even in cases where children were members of the households, single room rentals were declared as housing type, which could have directly impacted on the living conditions through lack of privacy, overcrowding, etc.

Access to Basic Services

Cases where water access was difficult were mostly related to informal settlements and/or occupied lands as living grounds.


There were cases where the connection to the network was done irregularly or illegally, and service cuts took place on occasions. Moreover, some households reported access to pre-paid options for accessing the electricity services. 

Pandemic Impacts on Access to Housing
The greatest impact the COVID-19 context and quarantine measures had on the surveyed households was debt accumulation, where 83% of respondents declared facing this problem. This was evidenced by the 74% of surveyed households that declared they skipped paying rent during the previous 3 months in order to be able to meet other needs. 

Out of the surveyed sample, 21% of households declared to have experienced threats of eviction during the COVID-19 context, with 8% having been evicted during the health crisis declared nationwide. At the time of the survey, 20% of the interviewees declared facing risk of eviction during the following month(s), mostly due to lack of ability to pay rent. 

At the time of the survey, 72% of respondents declared not planning to move or change their place of residence in the following months. However, their decision was mostly conditioned to the debt accumulation and their lack of payment ability, which forced the permanence in the existing living situation (until outstanding amounts were paid entirely or partially).

The decision to relocate or move of the 19% of the respondents related to their need to find more affordable living spaces/rentals or to improve their declared economic situation. 66% stated wanting to stay in Panama, but change their area of residence (town, city or province); 26% of those considering relocation were planning to leave Panama and move to a third country (18%) or return to their CoO (8%) in order to reach a favorable solution for the existing lack of income and/or working or residence rights, delays in the regularization of their migratory status, and/or difficult economic situation faced in Panama. 7% mentioned wanting to relocate or move, but did not provide further details on the area, country, or region of interest.

9% of surveyed persons did not know if they would move in the following months mostly because the decision was linked to received eviction threats by the lessor.

Access to Livelihoods

Employment

Half of the interviewees (50%) reported being unemployed at the time of the survey, from which the highest percentages were represented by nationals of Nicaragua, Colombia and Venezuela. Moreover, although 33% of respondents reported to have had a formal employment contract in their CoO, only 4% of respondents were formally employed in Panama at the time of the survey.

Income Generation
22.5% of the surveyed households did not record a single paid hour during the entire month.

Before the COVID-19 context, 98% of households depended on earnings from an economic activity to cover their basic needs, and only 20% relied on some form of humanitarian assistance. Furthermore, 13% of households declared to have had savings before the restrictions imposed due to the declared COVID-19 pandemic.

At the time of the survey, 91% of respondents reported their work situation to have been impacted by the restrictions due to the COVID-19 pandemic, on which the greatest consequences were reflected in the labor circumstances of Honduran, Colombian and Salvadoran populations. 

Access to Health

More than three-quarters of surveyed households (78%) declared that when needed – before or during COVID-19 context – they could go to a medical health care center and had access to medical attention. When addressed with the issue of healthcare access, respondents reported that the difficulty arouse when needing to follow up on the treatment or lab tests due to the high costs they entail. 

Only about one-third of all declared COVID-19 positive cases reported to have received assistance and aid from the government. In cases where interviewees did not receive the assistance (food and medicine) from the State, the church or community members offered the support to the household and thus their needs were met during the quarantine period.

Access to Education

Enrollment Rate and Access to Virtual Education

In the case of families with children, 34% of the surveyed cases declared they did not have the necessary resources and access for the children in the household to receive their education virtually (during the school year 2020). The lowest level of access was found amongst the Nicaraguan nationals, while the highest level of access was reached by the respondents of Colombian nationality, followed by Venezuelans. Nonetheless, 89% of households reported that their children continued their studies during the restrictions imposed due to the COVID-19 context. That was mainly possible due to the assistance provided by UNHCR and partners. Many interviewees reported to have received connectivity support (pre-paid phone cards for Internet access) and smart devices (tablets) that enabled students to continue receiving classes remotely.

Virtual Classes Methodology

Most households reported a combination of various methods when describing the means through which education was delivered during the COVID-19 context.

Digital Connectivity

Roughly half of the surveyed households (54%) declared having easy or very easy access to the Internet when needed, while a little more than a third (37%) of interviews reflected difficult or very difficult access to the Internet when needed. 9% of the surveyed persons remain neutral as they depend on the purchasing power of the moment for the buying of pre-paid cards. 

Social Cohesion and Community Integration

Although 84% of interviewees reported feeling safe in the area where they live, 13% disagreed or strongly disagreed. Through the applied surveys it was known that the crimes of which the respondents have been victims in Panama have increased in severity, as they have experienced burglaries and/or robberies, while witnessing violent acts such as murders and executions, drug dealings, gang and criminal groups’ activity, and an increased numbers of threats. The Colombian, Nicaraguan, and Salvadorian populations show the highest level of perception of risk and unsafety in their communities or areas of residence.

Many respondents declared that their daily habits and behaviors did not include social or leisure activities in communities’ public spaces, which resulted in rather isolated lifestyles, revolving around basic needs and services, with little interaction outside the household.


Moreover, more than one-third of respondents said they have been discriminated for their foreign status, regardless of their specific nationality. These reports are also validated by findings presented in other social cohesion analysis reports carried out by UNHCR and IOM during 2020., which state that “more than half of refugees and migrants have felt discriminated against in the country” and that “78% of refugees and migrants said they had witnessed acts of discrimination in Panama against a foreign persons”. 

Despite the high percentages of declared perceptions of social isolation and/or discrimination, 79% of the surveyed persons stated they considered to have positive or very positive relationships and interactions with the Panamanians through establishment of close friendships and/or solidary acquaintances, especially during the COVID-19 health crisis context.

Impact of Quarantine Measures on Coexistence and Mental Health

Impact on Mental Health
The prolonged confinement, mobility restrictions, curfews and other sanitary measures imposed by the Panamanian government did not only impact the economy and access to basic needs and services. The COVID-19 pandemic has had an enormous impact on social interactions, family dynamics and personal wellbeing. The mental health issues that surfaced during this health crisis context were evidenced by respondents’ reports, partners’ support programs and State’s actions directed at mitigating risks. 

However, only 12% of surveyed households declared that they needed psychosocial or psychiatric care and/or support during the COVID-19 pandemic context, mostly of Honduran nationality, followed with much lower percentages by Venezuelans, Colombians, and Nicaraguans. The low numbers of surveyed persons who have accessed psycho-emotional support programs may not be a true reflection of the need for mental health services, but of the stigma this topic still holds.

Humanitarian Aid and Government Social Programs

Partners’ humanitarian assistance programs, as well as the support and interventions provided, reflected the priority needs identified by the respondents at the time of the survey amid the COVID-19 health crisis. Interviewees received different types of assistance or aid during the COVID-19 health crisis, mostly in the form of supermarket vouchers (used mainly for purchases of food, medicine, cleaning and hygiene supplies, and household items), pre-packed food packages, or shared meals from community members.

Sources of Assistance

Comparative: Types of Assistance

Before the pandemic, almost no interviewee (only 0.2%) stated to have received government assistance of any kind. Additionally, respondents reported they had received assistance or support from a Partner, UN agency or other NGO in a slightly greater number (56%) before the COVID-19 context; however, the type and duration of the received assistance were different.


Respondents identified several Partners, NGOs and other UN agencies that had actively offered aid and support during the COVID-19 context: RET International (in collaboration with the Food Bank) provided food packages; the Pastoral of Human Mobility (sometimes identified by the interviewees as the Catholic Church) received food packages from Government aid programs, which were then delivered to the PoCs; the International Organization for Migration (IOM) offered food packages to surveyed households, especially to those that could not access other humanitarian aid programs due to the lack of regularization of their migratory status; the Panamanian Red Cross, HIAS and NRC provided food vouchers and CBI for rent payments, medicine and/or non-food essential items; community members (such as acquaintances, friends, and neighbors) also lent a helping hand when respondents faced urgent needs, and provided support with smaller scale donations (mainly of food, baby items, and essential non-food items, such as personal hygiene and cleaning supplies).

In terms of services provided, interviewees mentioned in many cases that they had received psychosocial support from Partners such as HIAS, the Panamanian Red Cross, and RET International; legal assistance and representation, from NRC; digital connectivity assistance from HIAS and RET International; and access to training opportunities from HIAS.
